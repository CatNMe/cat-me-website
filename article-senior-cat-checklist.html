Senior Cat Health Checklist

Older cats hide illness even better than younger ones. This practical checklist covers the vet visits, home checks, and small daily changes that keep your senior cat comfortable and help you catch problems early.

⏱️ 11 min read
Updated 7 July 2026
First published 5 July 2026
Reviewed for Cat&Me Health

Thanks to better food, indoor living and modern veterinary care, cats now regularly live into their late teens and beyond. Vets generally consider a cat “senior” from around 10 years old, and “geriatric” from about 15. This is the stage where a little extra attention pays off enormously.

Ageing itself isn’t a disease — but older cats are far more likely to develop conditions like kidney disease, arthritis, dental disease and an overactive thyroid, and they’re experts at hiding them. Use the checklist below as a simple routine to stay one step ahead.

💡 The single most important habit

See your vet more often. Guidelines recommend a check-up at least every 6 months for cats aged 10–15, and every 4 months for cats over 15. Twice-yearly visits catch quiet conditions long before you’d notice them at home.

1. Veterinary care & screening

Older cats benefit from “wellness testing” — routine check-ups and blood/urine tests that spot hidden disease early, while it’s most treatable.

  • Wellness exam every 6 months (every 4 months if over 15)
  • Annual blood work and urine test to screen kidneys, thyroid & diabetes
  • Blood pressure check (high blood pressure is common in older cats)
  • Dental assessment at each visit
  • Keep vaccinations and parasite control up to date per your vet’s advice
  • Record your cat’s weight at each visit to track trends

2. Weight, appetite & nutrition

Weight change is one of the clearest early warnings in a senior cat — in either direction. Weight loss can signal kidney disease, thyroid problems or diabetes; weight gain brings its own risks.

What to feed & watch

DailyVet-guided

Diet: a complete, good-quality senior diet with high-quality protein. Calorie needs often drop with age, but some very old cats actually need more to hold their weight — ask your vet which applies to your cat.

Meals: offer smaller, more frequent meals, since older cats often eat less at a sitting.

Watch for: any steady weight loss or gain, a suddenly bigger or smaller appetite, or difficulty eating — all worth a call to the vet.

3. Hydration

Because kidney function often declines with age, easy access to water matters more than ever. Dehydration makes many senior conditions worse.

  • Provide several clean, fresh water bowls around the home
  • Place water where a stiff cat can reach it easily (avoid high jumps)
  • Consider a pet water fountain — many cats drink more from moving water
  • Mix in or offer wet food to boost water intake

4. Mobility & arthritis

Arthritis is very common in older cats and easy to miss, because cats show it by doing less rather than by limping. A cat that has “slowed down” may actually be in pain.

Watch for: hesitating before jumping, using furniture as “steps,” struggling with stairs, sleeping more, reduced grooming, or grumpiness when touched.

  • Add ramps or steps to favourite perches and the bed
  • Use litter trays with at least one low side for easy entry
  • Provide soft, supportive bedding in warm, draught-free spots
  • Keep food, water and litter on the same floor to limit stairs
  • Ask your vet about safe pain relief — never give human painkillers

5. Dental & grooming care

Dental disease is common and genuinely painful, yet many cats keep eating through it. Older cats also groom less effectively, so their coat and claws need more help.

  • Check for bad breath, red gums, drooling or dropping food
  • Brush teeth with cat-safe toothpaste if your cat tolerates it
  • Brush the coat gently to prevent mats, especially long-haired cats
  • Check and trim claws regularly — senior claws overgrow and thicken
  • Look over the skin for lumps, sores or matting while grooming

6. Comfort & the home environment

Small changes make daily life much easier for an ageing cat, and support declining senses.

  • Keep warm, cosy resting spots away from draughts (warm, not hot)
  • Keep routines and furniture consistent — helpful for fading sight/hearing
  • Use night lights to help cats with reduced vision move around
  • Minimise stress and big changes where you can
  • Keep gently engaging with play and attention to support the mind

✓ Watch for cognitive changes

Older cats can develop cognitive decline — signs include night-time yowling, disorientation, wandering, or avoiding contact. It’s common in very old cats and worth mentioning to your vet, who can often help.

Signs that mean “call the vet”

In a senior cat, don’t “wait and see” with these — older cats become dehydrated and unwell faster than young ones.

🚨 Contact your vet if you notice

  • Sudden weight loss or gain
  • Drinking or urinating much more
  • Appetite drop or trouble eating
  • Repeated vomiting or diarrhoea
  • Difficulty jumping or moving
  • Increased hiding or confusion
  • New lumps or non-healing sores
  • Laboured breathing or collapse

When in doubt, phone your vet and describe what’s changed — early action makes senior conditions far easier to manage.

✓ The takeaway

Senior cats can thrive with a little extra care. Know your cat’s normal, do a quick monthly once-over, keep water and comfort easy to reach, and don’t skip those twice-yearly vet visits — they’re the single best way to add happy, comfortable years.
