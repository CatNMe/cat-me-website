Why Cat Eyes Glow in the Dark

Catch your cat in torchlight or a camera flash and its eyes blaze back at you like tiny lanterns. That eerie glow is no trick — it's a clever piece of night-vision hardware called the tapetum lucidum.

⏱️ 6 min read
Updated 8 July 2026
First published 8 July 2026
Reviewed for Cat&Me

Cats' eyes glow because of the tapetum lucidum — a reflective layer behind the retina that bounces light back through the eye, giving the light-sensing cells a second chance to catch it. It's what makes cats such superb low-light hunters.

💡 Quick summary

The tapetum lucidum is a mirror-like layer behind a cat's retina. It reflects light that the eye missed the first time back onto the light-sensitive cells, boosting night vision. The leftover light escaping the eye is the “eyeshine” you see. Cats see far better than us in low light, but not in total darkness.

1. The mirror behind the eye

Behind a cat's retina sits a thin, reflective layer called the tapetum lucidum — Latin for “bright carpet.” When light enters the eye, some of it lands on the retina's light-sensing cells straight away. The rest passes through and hits the tapetum, which reflects it back for a second pass.

This effectively doubles the light available to the retina, letting cats make the most of very dim conditions. Many other animals — dogs, deer, cattle — have one too, which is why their eyes shine in headlights.

2. Why it evolved

Cats are crepuscular — most active at dawn and dusk, when light is scarce and prey is on the move. The tapetum, combined with a large cornea and a pupil that opens very wide, makes their eyes brilliant at gathering light.

The trade-off is a slight loss of sharpness: reflected light can scatter a little, so images aren't quite as crisp as ours in bright daylight. For a hunter in the gloom, that's a worthwhile deal.

✓ Roughly how much better?

Cats are often said to need only a fraction of the light humans do to see — a big advantage at night. But no cat can see in complete darkness: without at least some light to reflect, even the tapetum has nothing to work with.

3. Why the glow comes in different colours

Eyeshine isn't always the same shade. The colour depends on the cat, its eye colour, age and the makeup of the tapetum:

Eyeshine colourOften seen in
Green / yellow-greenThe most common glow in adult cats
Golden or amberMany cats, varying with lighting angle
Blue or reddishSometimes in kittens, or blue-eyed cats with less pigment

4. Why it shows up in photos

A camera flash fires a burst of light straight into the eye. In cats, it reflects off the tapetum and back out toward the lens — producing that bright glow. In humans, who lack a tapetum, the flash instead reflects off the blood-rich retina, giving the familiar “red-eye” effect.

✓ Photo tip

To reduce glowing eyes in cat photos, avoid a direct flash. Use soft, indirect or natural light and photograph slightly off to the side rather than straight on.

5. When eyeshine is worth a second look

A healthy glow is normal. But changes can occasionally hint at an eye problem, so it's worth a vet check if you notice:

  • One eye that suddenly reflects very differently from the other
  • A cloudy, dull or milky appearance instead of a clear shine
  • Redness, squinting, discharge or signs of pain around the eye
  • New clumsiness or bumping into things in low light

💡 Note

These signs don't always mean something is wrong, but eyes are delicate. When in doubt, a quick vet check is the safest choice.

The takeaway

✓ In short

That night-time glow is your cat's tapetum lucidum at work — a built-in light amplifier that makes it a formidable low-light hunter. It's a normal, healthy feature; just keep an eye out for sudden changes.
