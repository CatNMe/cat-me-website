Behaviour & Body Language

Signs Your Cat Trusts You

Cats rarely gush. Their affection is subtle, quiet, and easy to miss — but it's absolutely real. Here are the behaviours that show your cat feels safe with you, and how to earn even more of that trust.

⏱️ 7 min read
Updated 7 July 2026
First published 7 July 2026
Reviewed for Cat&Me Behaviour

Cats form genuine bonds with their people — research on cat–human relationships shows they can be securely attached to us, much like dogs or even children are to caregivers. They just express it in a quieter language. Once you learn the signals, you'll notice your cat has been saying “I trust you” all along.

💡 The short version

Top signs of trust: slow blinks, an exposed belly, kneading, head bunting and rubbing, following you around, and choosing to sleep on or near you. The more relaxed and vulnerable your cat is willing to be, the more they trust you.

1. The slow blink

If your cat looks at you and slowly closes and opens their eyes, you're receiving one of the clearest signs of feline trust and affection — often called a “cat kiss.” In the wild, closing your eyes near another animal means letting your guard down, so a slow blink says “I feel safe with you.”

✓ Try this

Slow-blink back. Look softly at your cat, then slowly close and open your own eyes. Many cats will return the gesture — a tiny, silent conversation of trust.

2. Showing you their belly

The belly is a cat's most vulnerable spot — it protects all the vital organs. When a cat rolls over and exposes their tummy, they're signalling they feel completely relaxed and unthreatened around you. It's a profound sign of trust.

One important caveat: for most cats this is a display of trust, not always an invitation to rub the belly. Plenty of cats will happily grab your hand if you accept the “offer.” Appreciate the gesture, and judge your own cat before diving in.

3. Kneading (“making biscuits”)

That rhythmic push-push of the paws into your lap or a blanket is a leftover kitten behaviour — nursing kittens knead to stimulate their mother's milk. When an adult cat kneads on you, often while purring, they're associating you with the safety and comfort of that early bond. It's your cat saying you're part of their safe space.

4. Head bunting and cheek rubbing

When a cat bumps their head against you or rubs their cheeks on your hand or legs, they're depositing their scent from glands in the face. This marks you as “theirs” and part of their family group — a friendly, affectionate way of blending your scents together. It's a real vote of belonging.

5. Following you and sleeping nearby

A cat who trails you from room to room, sits where they can keep an eye on you, or chooses to nap on or beside you is showing attachment. Sleep is when animals are most defenceless, so a cat that dozes off against you clearly feels secure. A tail-up “hello” as you walk in is another warm greeting — a raised, upright tail is a confident, friendly signal.

6. Other quiet signs

  • Grooming you — licking your hand or hair is social grooming reserved for trusted companions.
  • “Chatting” — chirps, trills and soft meows aimed at you are attempts to communicate.
  • Bringing you “gifts” — toys (or, less charmingly, prey) can be a sharing behaviour.
  • Relaxed body language — soft eyes, ears forward, whiskers loose, tail calm.

How to build more trust

Trust grows through predictability and respect — never by forcing interaction.

  • Let your cat set the pace; allow them to approach you rather than grabbing them.
  • Keep routines steady — regular feeding and calm handling build security.
  • Respect “no”: if they move away or flick their tail, give them space.
  • Use play and treats to create positive associations with you.
  • Provide safe retreats (like boxes and high perches) so they always feel in control.

✓ The takeaway

A cat's trust is understated but genuine. Slow blinks, exposed bellies, kneading and quiet companionship all say the same thing: you're safe, and you're mine. Honour it by letting your cat lead.
