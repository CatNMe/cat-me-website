What to Feed a Kitten

Kittens grow astonishingly fast, and their diet has to keep up. Here's what to feed at each age, how much and how often, the wet-vs-dry question, and the foods to keep well away.

⏱️ 8 min read
Updated 7 July 2026
First published 7 July 2026
Reviewed for Cat&Me Kitten Care

A growing kitten burns through a huge amount of energy — at around 10 weeks old they need roughly two and a half times more energy per kilogram of body weight than an adult cat. That's why kittens need food made specifically for them, and why little-and-often feeding works best.

💡 The short version

Feed a complete kitten food (not adult food) until 12 months. Offer small, frequent meals — about 3–4 a day when young — always with fresh water. Follow the amounts on the pack, adjust to your kitten, and never give cow's milk.

Why kitten food, not adult food?

Foods labelled “complete and balanced for kittens” (or “for growth / all life stages”) are higher in energy, protein and key nutrients that rapid growth demands. Adult food simply doesn't provide enough for a developing kitten. Keep your kitten on kitten food until they reach about 12 months, then transition to adult food.

Feeding by age

Under 4 weeks

Kittens live entirely on their mother's milk, or a kitten milk replacer if orphaned — never cow's milk. Newborns feed very frequently, around the clock.

4–5 weeks (weaning begins)

Weaning starts around 4–5 weeks. Offer a gruel of wet kitten food mixed with a little warm water (or formula), gradually making it thicker as they take to it.

6–8 weeks

Kittens can eat wet food or moistened kibble reliably. By 8 weeks most are fully weaned and eating solid kitten food. This is around when kittens usually go to new homes.

2–6 months

The big growth phase. Feed 3–4 meals a day of complete kitten food, following the pack guide for amounts by weight and age.

6–12 months

You can ease down to 2–3 meals a day. Keep feeding kitten food until the first birthday, then switch gradually to adult food.

How much and how often

The most reliable amounts are printed on your kitten food's packaging, based on age and weight — always start there. Then watch your individual kitten: they should be lean and growing steadily, not skinny or pot-bellied.

✓ Make mealtimes calm

Cats like to eat alone in a quiet spot where they won't be startled. In multi-cat homes, feed kittens separately so each one gets their full portion.

Wet, dry, or both?

Both can be excellent as long as they're complete kitten foods. Many owners offer a mix:

  • Wet food adds moisture (great for hydration) and is easy for little mouths.
  • Dry food is convenient and can be left out for grazing.
  • Offering both early helps your kitten accept different textures later in life.

Change foods gradually over about a week — mixing increasing amounts of the new into the old — to avoid tummy upsets.

Water, milk and treats

  • Fresh water should always be available — keep it away from the food and litter tray.
  • No cow's milk. Most cats are lactose intolerant and it can cause diarrhoea. Water is all they need to drink.
  • Treats in moderation — keep them to no more than about 5% of daily intake so the balanced diet does the heavy lifting.

⚠️ Foods to avoid

Keep kittens away from onions, garlic, chocolate, caffeine, alcohol, grapes and raisins, and don't rely on human meat scraps as a meal. If your kitten has ongoing vomiting, diarrhoea, or won't eat, contact your vet.

✓ The takeaway

Complete kitten food, small frequent meals, fresh water, and patience with gradual changes — that's the whole recipe for a healthy, well-grown kitten. Follow the pack, watch your kitten, and check in with your vet at routine visits.
