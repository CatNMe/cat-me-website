10 Signs Your Cat May Be Sick

Cats are hard-wired to hide illness, so the first clues are usually small changes in their normal routine. Here are the ten signs worth watching — what each one can mean, and when a change should send you to the vet.

⏱️ 9 min read
Updated 7 July 2026
First published 2 July 2026
Reviewed for Cat&Me Health

In the wild, a cat that looks weak or unwell is an easy target. Even though our cats live safely indoors, that survival instinct hasn’t gone away — so they tend to mask pain and illness until they feel very unwell. That’s why owners often say their cat “suddenly” became sick, when in reality the signs were building quietly for days or weeks.

The good news: you know your cat’s normal behaviour better than anyone. This guide walks through the ten most useful warning signs, explains what each can mean, and tells you when a change is worth a phone call — and when it’s a true emergency.

💡 The golden rule

You don’t need to diagnose your cat — you just need to notice change. Any sudden or unusual change in appearance, behaviour or habits that lasts more than 24–48 hours is a reason to call your vet.

1.Changes in appetite or thirst

A cat that skips the odd meal isn’t unusual, but a cat that stops eating for more than 24 hours, or eats noticeably less for several days, needs attention. Refusing food is one of the most common early signs of illness — and in cats it can quickly become dangerous.

Watch drinking too. Suddenly drinking a lot more water is a classic early sign of conditions such as kidney disease, diabetes or an overactive thyroid, especially in older cats. Drinking much less than usual matters as well.

⚠️ Don’t “wait it out” with food

A cat that eats nothing for 2–3 days can develop a serious liver condition called hepatic lipidosis. If your cat refuses all food for more than 24 hours, call your vet.

2.Hiding or unusual behaviour changes

A cat that suddenly hides under the bed, avoids the family, or stops greeting you is often telling you something is wrong. The opposite can also be a sign: a normally independent cat that becomes unusually clingy or restless.

Because behaviour change is so easy to explain away (“she’s just in a mood”), it’s one of the most overlooked signs — yet it’s frequently the very first clue that a cat is in pain or feeling ill.

3.Sleeping more or very low energy

Cats sleep a lot — often 12 to 16 hours a day — so the key is a change from their normal level. A cat that’s suddenly lethargic, reluctant to jump up to favourite spots, or hard to rouse for play and meals may be unwell.

Reduced jumping and climbing can also be a subtle sign of joint pain or arthritis, which is very common in older cats but easy to miss.

4.Litter tray changes

The litter tray is one of the most useful health monitors you have. Note any change in how often your cat goes, and what it looks like:

  • Straining, frequent trips, or crying in the tray — possible urinary problem.
  • Blood in urine or faeces.
  • Diarrhoea or constipation lasting more than a day or two.
  • Going outside the tray when they normally don’t.

⚠️ Emergency: a male cat straining to urinate

A cat (especially a male) who strains repeatedly but produces little or no urine may have a blocked bladder. This is life-threatening and needs a vet immediately — within hours, not days.

5.Vomiting or diarrhoea

An occasional hairball isn’t a crisis. But repeated vomiting, vomiting that won’t stop, or diarrhoea lasting more than 24–48 hours can lead to dehydration and points to an underlying problem — from diet and parasites to more serious illness.

Call your vet sooner if you also see blood, if your cat seems painful or weak, or if they can’t keep water down.

6.Weight loss or weight gain

Because cats are small, even a little weight change is significant. Gradual weight loss — even in a cat that seems to be eating well — is a common sign of thyroid disease, diabetes, dental pain or other conditions. You may feel the spine and ribs becoming more prominent.

Weighing your cat once a month (weigh yourself holding them, then subtract your own weight) is a simple way to catch changes early.

7.Changes in coat and grooming

A healthy cat grooms itself and keeps a smooth, glossy coat. Watch for:

  • A dull, greasy or matted coat — a sign the cat has stopped grooming, which often means they feel unwell.
  • Over-grooming or bald patches — which can signal stress, pain, allergies or skin problems.
  • Increased shedding, dandruff or sores.

8.Eyes, nose and mouth changes

Runny eyes, discharge from the nose, sneezing or a visible “third eyelid” across the eye can indicate infection. Check the mouth too: bad breath, drooling, red gums or difficulty eating often mean dental disease, which affects a large proportion of adult cats and can be genuinely painful.

✓ Quick gum check

Healthy gums are pink and moist. Pale, white, blue or bright-red gums are a warning sign — pale or blue gums are an emergency.

9.Changes in vocalisation

A normally quiet cat that starts crying, yowling or meowing much more — particularly at night or in the litter tray — may be in pain, confused, or unwell. In older cats, increased night-time calling can be linked to high blood pressure, thyroid problems or cognitive decline.

If new, persistent vocalising lasts more than a day or two, mention it to your vet.

10.Breathing difficulty

Cats rarely pant, so any laboured, fast or open-mouthed breathing is serious. Cats in respiratory distress often go quiet and hide rather than making a fuss, so it’s easy to miss.

⚠️ This is an emergency

Signs include an outstretched neck, elbows held away from the body, exaggerated chest movement, open-mouth breathing or panting, and blue-tinged gums. Go to a vet immediately.

🚨 Signs that need a vet straight away

  • Difficulty or open-mouth breathing
  • Collapse or unresponsiveness
  • Seizures
  • Straining but unable to urinate
  • Pale, white or blue gums
  • Repeated vomiting of blood
  • Sudden inability to walk or use back legs
  • Known contact with a poison (e.g. lilies, medication)

If in doubt, phone your vet or an emergency clinic and describe what you’re seeing — they can help you decide how urgently your cat needs to be seen.

How to help your vet help your cat

When you call or visit, a few notes make diagnosis faster and easier:

  1. When the change started and whether it’s getting worse.
  2. Eating and drinking — how much, compared with normal.
  3. Litter tray — frequency, appearance, any straining or blood.
  4. Weight — any recent changes you’ve noticed or measured.
  5. A short phone video of anything unusual (breathing, walking, vocalising).

✓ Prevention beats cure

Routine vet check-ups — at least yearly for adults and twice a year for senior cats — catch many problems before they become serious. Keeping a rough note of your cat’s normal weight, appetite and habits makes changes much easier to spot.

The bottom line

Cats are experts at hiding illness, so your best tool is simply knowing what “normal” looks like for your cat. Trust your instincts: if something seems off and stays off for a day or two, it’s always worth a call. For breathing trouble, collapse, seizures, or a cat straining to urinate, don’t wait — seek help immediately.
