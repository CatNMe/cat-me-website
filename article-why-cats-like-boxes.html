Why Cats Like Boxes

Buy your cat an expensive bed and they'll ignore it for the cardboard box it arrived in. It's not random — boxes tap into some deep feline instincts, and science backs up why cats find them irresistible.

⏱️ 6 min read
Updated 7 July 2026
First published 7 July 2026
Reviewed for Cat&Me Behaviour

A cardboard box is basically the perfect cat product: cheap, disposable, and packed with everything a cat instinctively wants. To understand the obsession, it helps to remember that your cat is a small predator who is also, in the wild, potential prey — and a snug box answers both sides of that nature at once.

💡 The short version

Boxes give cats security, warmth, and a hiding/ambush spot. Research even shows boxes measurably reduce stress in cats adjusting to new environments — and cats are drawn to enclosed “box-like” shapes almost automatically.

1. Security and stress relief

The biggest reason is safety. An enclosed space with solid sides means nothing can sneak up from behind — a cat can watch the world from a spot where it feels protected. Hiding is one of a cat's main ways of coping with stress, and a box provides an instant hiding place.

This isn't just anecdotal. In a well-known study of newly arrived cats in a Dutch animal shelter, cats given a hiding box adjusted far faster — their stress scores dropped more quickly and reached a lower, calmer baseline about a week sooner than cats without one. Later reviews of hospitalised cats found similar results: hiding boxes are associated with reduced fear and stress. A box is genuinely therapeutic.

✓ Try this

Bringing home a new cat, or dealing with a nervous one? Leave a plain cardboard box on its side in a quiet corner. It gives your cat a safe base to retreat to and settle from — one of the simplest calming tools there is.

2. Warmth

Cats are comfiest at a warmer temperature than most people keep their homes. Cardboard is a surprisingly good insulator, and a snug box traps a cat's own body heat, creating a cosy pocket. A tight, enclosed space simply holds warmth better than an open bed — so the box wins again.

3. The ambush hunter instinct

Cats are ambush predators. In the wild they hunt by hiding, watching, and pouncing from cover. A box is an ideal launch pad: your cat can tuck inside, stay unseen, and spring out at a toy, a housemate, or your unsuspecting ankles. That “peeking out of the box before an attack” pose is pure predatory instinct at play — and part of why boxes are such good enrichment.

4. “If I fits, I sits” — even fake boxes

Cats are so drawn to enclosed shapes that they'll sit in outlines of boxes that aren't really there. In a citizen-science study cheekily titled “If I fits I sits,” researchers found cats would settle inside 2-D square shapes and even optical illusions of squares taped to the floor — no walls required. The instinct to occupy a defined, enclosed-feeling space is that strong.

Keeping box time safe

  • Remove staples, tape and plastic strips that a cat could chew or swallow.
  • Leave the flaps open or cut a second opening so your cat can never feel trapped.
  • Give shy or multi-cat households more than one box, so no one gets cornered.
  • Swap boxes out when they get grubby — they're free to replace.

✓ The takeaway

Boxes aren't a silly quirk — they satisfy a cat's need for safety, warmth and cover all at once, and they measurably lower stress. So keep a box or two around. It's the cheapest enrichment your cat will ever love.
