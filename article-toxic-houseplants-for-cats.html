Toxic Houseplants for Cats

Many popular houseplants and cut flowers are poisonous to cats — and a few, like lilies, can be fatal from the tiniest nibble. Here's what to keep out, what to watch for, and safer greenery you can enjoy instead.

⏱️ 9 min read
Updated 8 July 2026
First published 8 July 2026
Reviewed for Cat&Me Safety

A leafy home looks lovely, but for a curious cat some of the most common houseplants and bouquets are genuinely dangerous. A few cause nothing worse than an upset stomach; others can trigger mouth pain, breathing trouble, or life-threatening kidney failure. The single most important thing to know: true lilies are deadly to cats, and the safest response to almost any plant poisoning is a fast call to your vet. Here's a practical rundown of what to avoid and what you can safely grow.

⚠️ Lilies are a feline emergency

True lilies (Easter, Tiger, Stargazer, Asiatic, Day lilies and others) are extremely toxic to cats. Every part — petals, leaves, pollen, even the vase water — can cause fatal kidney failure, and just brushing pollen onto their fur and grooming it off can be enough. The safest option is to never bring lilies into a home with cats. If exposure is suspected, treat it as an emergency and call your vet immediately.

The most dangerous plants

These are the plants and flowers most worth keeping out of a cat household entirely.

🌺 True lilies & day lilies

Why: Cause acute kidney failure in cats even in tiny amounts. Watch for: vomiting, drooling, loss of appetite, lethargy, then reduced or increased urination. Action: emergency vet, same hour. Note that peace lily and calla lily are not “true” lilies — they're less deadly but still irritating (see below).

🌿 Sago palm

Why: All parts are highly toxic and can cause liver failure; the seeds are the most poisonous. Watch for: vomiting, diarrhoea, weakness, jaundice. Action: emergency vet.

🌷 Spring bulbs — daffodils, tulips, hyacinths & amaryllis

Why: The bulbs are the most toxic part and can cause vomiting, drooling, heart and breathing problems in larger doses. Watch for: intense drooling, vomiting, tremors. Keep both potted bulbs and bouquets out of reach.

🍂 Azalea & rhododendron

Why: Contain grayanotoxins affecting the heart. Watch for: vomiting, drooling, weakness, abnormal heart rhythm, collapse. Action: vet promptly, even for small amounts.

Common irritant houseplants

These are hugely popular and mostly cause mouth and stomach irritation rather than organ failure — because they contain insoluble calcium oxalate crystals that act like tiny needles when chewed. Rarely life-threatening, but genuinely painful, so best kept out of reach.

PlantTypical effect if chewed
Pothos / Devil's ivyMouth & tongue irritation, drooling, pawing at mouth, vomiting
Philodendron & MonsteraOral pain, drooling, difficulty swallowing
Peace lily & Calla lilyBurning mouth, drooling, vomiting (not the deadly “true lily” kidney effect)
Dieffenbachia (Dumb cane)Intense oral irritation, swelling, drooling
Elephant's ear (Alocasia)Mouth irritation, drooling, vomiting
Aloe veraVomiting, diarrhoea, lethargy
Snake plant & Jade plantVomiting, drooling, GI upset

💡 “Lily” in the name doesn't always mean deadly

Peace lilies and calla lilies are not true lilies and don't cause the kidney failure that Easter or Tiger lilies do — but they still contain irritating crystals. When in doubt, check the plant against the ASPCA toxic plant list rather than relying on the name.

Signs of plant poisoning

Because cats roam and hide, you may not see the nibble itself. Watch for these signs, which can appear immediately or be delayed a day or two:

  • Drooling, pawing at the mouth, or difficulty swallowing
  • Vomiting or diarrhoea — sometimes with bits of plant
  • Loss of appetite and low energy
  • Drinking and urinating more (or less) than usual
  • Red or swollen skin, mouth ulcers, or pale gums
  • Twitching, tremors, seizures, or collapse (severe cases)

These signs overlap with many other illnesses, so they don't prove poisoning — but combined with access to a toxic plant they warrant a call to your vet.

⚠️ If you suspect poisoning

Call your vet or an animal poison line straight away — don't wait for symptoms with lilies or sago palm. If you can, take a photo or a piece of the plant to help identify it. Do not try to make your cat vomit unless a vet tells you to.

Cat-safe plants & smart swaps

You don't have to give up greenery. These are widely considered non-toxic to cats (though any plant can cause mild tummy upset if gorged on):

  • Spider plant
  • Areca & parlour palm (true palms, unlike sago)
  • Boston fern & button fern
  • Calathea & prayer plant
  • African violet
  • Money plant (Pilea) and Peperomia
  • Cat grass & catnip — grown deliberately for nibbling

✓ The takeaway

Ban true lilies and sago palm from your home outright, keep irritant plants like pothos and philodendron well out of reach, and offer cat grass as a safe outlet for nibbling. When you're unsure about a plant, check the ASPCA list before buying — and call your vet fast if you suspect a nibble.
