How to Cat-Proof Your Home

Cats are climbers, chewers and expert escape artists. A short afternoon of cat-proofing — room by room — removes the everyday hazards that send cats to the vet, from dangling cords to open windows and hidden chemicals.

⏱️ 9 min read
Updated 8 July 2026
First published 8 July 2026
Reviewed for Cat&Me Safety

Cats explore with their paws, teeth and endless curiosity — which means the ordinary objects of daily life can become genuine hazards. The good news is that cat-proofing isn't complicated. The trick is to get down to their level, think like a small, agile predator that also loves hiding, and work through your home one room at a time. Here's a practical, room-by-room checklist.

💡 Think like a cat

Before you start, crouch down and scan each room low. Ask: what can be climbed, what dangles, what could be swallowed, and where could a curious cat hide or get trapped? That mindset catches most hazards fast.

Windows, balconies & high places

Cats love a high vantage point, but falls and escapes are real risks — even confident climbers misjudge.

  • Fit secure, sturdy screens on windows that will be opened.
  • Keep cats off balconies, upper decks and high open windows, or net/enclose them.
  • Tie up blind and curtain cords or switch to cordless — looped cords are a strangulation hazard.
  • Anchor bookshelves and tall, top-heavy furniture to the wall so they can't topple when climbed.
  • Keep breakables off shelves cats can reach — bored cats love to knock things down.

Strings, cords & small objects

Stringy and tiny items are among the most common causes of emergency surgery in cats.

⚠️ String can be deadly

Yarn, thread, dental floss, ribbon, hair ties, tinsel and elastic bands can bunch up and cut through the intestines if swallowed. If you see string coming from your cat's mouth or rear, don't pull it — go straight to your vet.

  • Store sewing kits, floss, ribbon and hair ties in closed drawers or containers.
  • Protect or hide electrical cords with cord covers to prevent chewing, shocks and burns.
  • Pick up small swallowable items: coins, rubber bands, small toys, jewellery, and building bricks.
  • Keep sewing needles, pins and safety pins strictly out of reach.

Chemicals, medicines & toxic foods

Many everyday household products are poisonous to cats — and cats are clever enough to open cupboards.

Keep locked awayWhy it matters
Cleaning products, bleach, detergentsCats are very sensitive to chemical irritants
AntifreezeDeadly — and its sweet taste attracts cats; clean spills immediately
Human & pet medicinesPainkillers like paracetamol are highly toxic to cats
Pesticides, rodent bait, mothballsHighly poisonous, sometimes with delayed effects
Toxic foods (onion, garlic, chocolate, grapes/raisins, alcohol, xylitol)Cause anaemia, organ damage or worse
  • Use child-proof latches on low cabinets storing chemicals, meds or sharp items.
  • Keep counters and dining tables clear of dangerous foods, and secure the bin.
  • Store button batteries safely — they cause severe burns if swallowed.

Appliances, hiding spots & plants

  • Always check before you shut or start washing machines, tumble dryers, dishwashers, ovens, fridges and reclining chairs — cats climb inside to hide or keep warm.
  • Close toilet lids (cleaner chemicals, and a drowning risk for kittens).
  • Cover or block gaps behind large appliances and under sofas where a cat could get stuck.
  • Remove or hang toxic houseplants well out of reach — especially lilies, which are deadly to cats.
  • Provide scratching posts to redirect claws away from furniture.

Quick cat-proofing checklist

  • Window screens secure; balconies off-limits or enclosed
  • Blind and curtain cords tied up or cordless
  • Tall furniture anchored to the wall
  • String, floss, ribbon and hair ties put away
  • Electrical cords covered or hidden
  • Chemicals and medicines behind childproof latches
  • Antifreeze spills cleaned immediately
  • Toxic foods and bins secured
  • Appliances checked before closing or starting
  • Toxic plants removed; scratching posts provided
  • Microchip and ID up to date in case of escape

✓ The takeaway

Cat-proofing is a one-off afternoon that pays off for years. Secure the high and dangling hazards, lock away chemicals and string, check appliances before you close them, and keep toxic plants and foods out of reach. Then re-scan whenever you rearrange a room.
