Why Is My Cat Vomiting?

Cats vomit for all sorts of reasons — some harmless, some serious. Here’s how to tell the difference, what the colour of the vomit can mean, and exactly when it’s time to call your vet.

⏱️ 10 min read
Updated 7 July 2026
First published 4 July 2026
Reviewed for Cat&Me Health

Almost every cat owner has stepped on a cold, damp surprise at some point. Vomiting is so common in cats that it’s easy to shrug off — but vets are clear on one thing: vomiting is common, yet it is not “normal.” It’s usually a sign of something, even if that something is minor.

The good news is that most one-off episodes are harmless. The key is knowing how to tell an occasional hairball from a pattern that needs a vet. This guide walks through the difference between vomiting and regurgitation, the most common causes, what the colour means, and the red flags that mean “call the vet now.”

💡 The quick rule of thumb

An occasional hairball or one-off sick-up in an otherwise happy, eating, playful cat is usually fine to monitor. Vomiting that is repeated, frequent, or paired with other symptoms (off food, tired, diarrhoea) needs a vet.

Vomiting vs. regurgitation

These look similar but mean different things, and your vet will want to know which you saw.

  • Vomiting is active. The cat heaves, its belly contracts, and what comes up is usually partly digested and may contain bile (yellow fluid). It often comes with drooling or nausea beforehand.
  • Regurgitation is passive. Food comes back up quickly and effortlessly, usually undigested and tube-shaped, with no heaving. This points more to the oesophagus than the stomach.

If you can, note which one it looked like, how long after eating it happened, and what the material looked like. A quick phone video is genuinely useful for your vet.

Common causes that are usually mild

Hairballs

Very commonManageable

The classic cause. As cats groom, they swallow loose hair that can collect in the stomach and be brought back up. More common in long-haired cats and heavy groomers.

Help at home: brush your cat regularly, offer a fibre-rich or hairball-control diet, and keep them well hydrated. But note: frequent hairballs (more than about one a week, or daily) can actually signal an underlying gut problem — mention it to your vet.

Eating too fast

Very commonEasy fix

Some cats bolt their food and bring it straight back up, undigested, minutes later. This is sometimes called “scarf and barf.”

Help at home: feed smaller, more frequent meals, use a slow-feeder bowl or puzzle feeder, and separate cats that compete at mealtimes.

Diet changes & dietary upsets

CommonPreventable

A sudden switch of food, spoiled or too-cold food, or eating something they shouldn’t (like grass or a houseplant) can all irritate the stomach.

Help at home: change foods gradually over about a week, serve wet food at room temperature, and keep toxic plants such as lilies out of the house.

An empty stomach (bile vomiting)

CommonManageable

If too long passes between meals, stomach acid and bile can build up and irritate the lining, bringing up yellow, foamy liquid — often first thing in the morning.

Help at home: splitting meals so the gap between them is shorter often helps. If it keeps happening, ask your vet.

Causes that need a vet

Vomiting can also be the first visible sign of a more serious problem, especially when it’s frequent or comes with other symptoms.

  • Intestinal parasites (worms) — you may even see worms in the vomit.
  • Foreign bodies — string, toys or other objects causing a blockage. This can be an emergency.
  • Kidney disease — a common cause in older cats.
  • Hyperthyroidism — an overactive thyroid in middle-aged and senior cats.
  • Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) or food intolerance — a frequent cause of chronic vomiting.
  • Pancreatitis or liver disease.
  • Infections, toxins, or reactions to medication.
  • Cancer — less common, but possible, particularly in older cats.

You don’t need to work out which one it is — that’s your vet’s job. Your job is to notice the pattern and the accompanying signs.

What the colour can tell you

Colour is a helpful clue, not a diagnosis — but it’s worth noting before you clean up.

What you seeWhat it may mean
Clump of hairHairball — usually mild if occasional
Undigested foodAte too fast, or regurgitation
Yellow / foamyBile, often from an empty stomach
Clear or white foamEmpty stomach or irritation
GreenBile, or eaten plant material
Bright red bloodActive bleeding — see a vet promptly
Brown, “coffee-ground” lookDigested blood — see a vet promptly

🚨 Call a vet straight away if

  • Vomiting more than 2–3 times in a row
  • Blood in the vomit (red or coffee-ground)
  • Can’t keep water down
  • Weakness, collapse or severe lethargy
  • Not eating for more than 24 hours
  • Vomiting plus diarrhoea together
  • Signs of dehydration (sunken eyes, dry gums)
  • Already has kidney disease, diabetes or hyperthyroidism

Very young kittens and senior cats become dehydrated fast — don’t wait as long before calling.

What you can do at home (for mild cases)

If your cat has vomited once, seems otherwise well, and has none of the red flags above, vets often suggest:

  1. Remove food for a short period (around 12 hours for an adult cat), but keep water available.
  2. Offer small sips of water or a few ice cubes so they stay hydrated.
  3. Reintroduce small amounts of a plain, bland food, then gradually return to normal if the vomiting has stopped.
  4. Watch closely for the next 24 hours for any of the warning signs.

⚠️ Don’t fast kittens or seniors

Withholding food is only for otherwise-healthy adult cats. Kittens, senior cats, and cats with existing conditions should be seen by a vet rather than fasted. Never give human anti-sickness medicines without vet advice.

Lowering the chances

  • Brush regularly to cut down on swallowed hair.
  • Feed measured meals and slow down fast eaters.
  • Change diets gradually, not overnight.
  • Keep toxic plants and swallowable objects (string, hair ties) out of reach.
  • Stay up to date with worming.
  • Keep up routine vet check-ups so conditions like kidney disease are caught early.

✓ The takeaway

One hairball from a happy, hungry cat is rarely a worry. Vomiting that repeats, or comes with a change in appetite, energy or litter tray habits, deserves a vet visit. When in doubt, phone your vet and describe what you saw.
