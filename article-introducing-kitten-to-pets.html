Introducing a Kitten to Pets

First impressions matter — a lot. Rushing a face-to-face meeting is the most common mistake. Go slow, let scent do the work first, and you'll set up a friendship that lasts instead of a rivalry.

⏱️ 8 min read
Updated 7 July 2026
First published 7 July 2026
Reviewed for Cat&Me Kitten Care

To your resident cat or dog, a new kitten first looks like an intruder, not a friend. The whole art of a good introduction is changing that first impression gradually — building positive associations through scent and controlled contact before anyone comes nose to nose. Depending on the animals, this can take a few days to a few months. Don't rush it.

💡 The short version

Keep the kitten fully separated in a safe room at first, swap scents, feed on both sides of the door, then do site swaps and barrier meetings before short, supervised face-to-face time. Move to the next step only when both animals are calm.

Before you start

  • Health check first. Make sure both pets are healthy and the kitten has been vet-checked for contagious illnesses before any contact.
  • Set up a safe room for the kitten with food, water, litter, bed and hiding spots — their own “base camp.”
  • Give the resident pet their normal territory and routine so they feel secure, not displaced.

The step-by-step process

Step 1 — Complete separation

For the first several days (often about a week), keep the kitten in their safe room with no direct contact. Both animals get used to the idea of each other through sounds and smells under the door, safely.

Step 2 — Scent swapping

Swap bedding, blankets or toys between the two, and stroke one then the other, to mix their scents. Let each sniff a cloth that smells of the other and pair it with treats so the new smell means good things.

Step 3 — Feed on either side of the door

Place both animals' food bowls near opposite sides of the closed door. Eating close together (but unseen) builds a positive association. Move bowls a little closer over days as they stay relaxed.

Step 4 — Site swapping

Let the kitten explore the rest of the home while the resident pet spends time in the kitten's room (and vice versa). They investigate each other's scent thoroughly without meeting, and shared spaces pick up a common smell.

Step 5 — Barrier introductions

Allow them to see each other through a baby gate or cracked door. Keep sessions short and calm, reward relaxed behaviour, and end before anyone gets stressed. Repeat until both are unbothered by the sight of each other.

Step 6 — Supervised face-to-face

Finally, allow brief supervised meetings in a shared space with easy escape routes and high perches. Keep them short and positive at first, gradually extending as trust grows. Never leave them alone together until you're confident.

If the resident pet is a dog

The same scent-first, gradual approach applies, with a few extras:

  • Keep the dog on a lead for early face-to-face meetings so you stay in control.
  • Reward the dog for calm behaviour and gently interrupt over-excited staring or lunging.
  • Always give the kitten an escape route and high spots the dog can't reach.
  • Never let a dog chase the kitten, even in play — it sets the relationship back.

Reading the room

Relaxed & readyStressed — slow down
Loose body, normal tailFlattened ears, puffed fur
Curious sniffingGrowling, hissing, spitting
Eating and playing normallyHiding, not eating, guarding
Calm, brief glancesHard staring, stalking, lunging

⚠️ Don't force it

Some hissing and wariness early on is normal, but real fighting is not. If you see persistent aggression, go back a step and slow down. A little patience now prevents a lasting feud.

✓ The takeaway

Separate, then let scent, food and controlled sightings build the friendship before any face-to-face meeting. Go at the animals' pace — not yours — and only progress when both stay calm.
