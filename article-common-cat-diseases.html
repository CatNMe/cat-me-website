Common Cat Diseases Explained

A plain-English guide to the health problems cats face most often — what causes them, the signs to watch for, how vets treat them, and what you can do to lower the risk.

⏱️ 11 min read
Updated 7 July 2026
First published 3 July 2026
Reviewed for Cat&Me Health

Cats are famously stoic. They tend to hide illness by instinct, which means the problems on this page often creep up gradually before an owner notices anything is wrong. The aim here isn’t to turn you into a vet — it’s to help you recognise the most common conditions, know which signs matter, and understand what treatment usually looks like.

We’ve grouped the diseases into three broad types: long-term (chronic) conditions that mostly affect older cats, infectious diseases spread between cats, and everyday problems like dental disease, obesity and parasites. None of this replaces a proper diagnosis — if your cat seems unwell, always call your vet.

💡 One theme runs through all of these

Early detection changes outcomes. Yearly check-ups for adult cats — and twice-yearly for seniors — catch many of these conditions before they become serious or costly.

Long-term (chronic) conditions

These tend to appear in middle-aged and older cats and are managed over months or years rather than cured.

Chronic kidney disease (CKD)

ChronicCommon in seniors

One of the most common diseases in older cats. The kidneys gradually lose their ability to filter waste and concentrate urine.

Watch for: drinking and urinating more, weight loss, reduced appetite, lethargy and vomiting.

Treatment: not curable, but a special kidney diet, good hydration, and medication can keep cats comfortable and slow the decline. Early diagnosis makes a big difference.

Hyperthyroidism

ChronicMiddle-aged & older

An overactive thyroid gland produces too much thyroid hormone, speeding up the metabolism. Usually caused by a benign (non-cancerous) swelling.

Watch for: weight loss despite a big appetite, increased thirst, restlessness or hyperactivity, a fast heart rate, and an unkempt or greasy coat.

Treatment: daily medication, a special diet, surgery, or radioactive iodine therapy — which can be curative. Left untreated it can harm the heart and other organs.

Diabetes mellitus

ChronicLinked to weight

The body can’t produce or respond to insulin properly, so blood sugar rises. More common in overweight and middle-aged cats.

Watch for: drinking a lot more, urinating more, increased appetite paired with weight loss, and lethargy.

Treatment: often managed well with insulin and diet. Caught early, some cats even go into remission. Weight control is the best prevention.

Arthritis

ChronicOften missed

Very common in older cats and easily overlooked because cats hide pain and simply move less.

Watch for: reluctance to jump, hesitating on stairs, sleeping more, reduced grooming, and irritability when touched.

Treatment: pain relief prescribed by a vet, weight management, and easy-access ramps, steps and soft bedding.

Infectious diseases

These spread between cats. Vaccination, keeping cats indoors, and reducing contact with unknown cats all lower the risk.

Cat flu (upper respiratory infection)

InfectiousVaccine available

Usually caused by feline herpesvirus or calicivirus. Very contagious between cats, especially in multi-cat homes and shelters.

Watch for: sneezing, runny nose and eyes, discharge, mouth ulcers, reduced appetite and lethargy.

Treatment: supportive care (fluids, nursing, keeping the nose and eyes clean); antibiotics only for secondary infections. Vaccination reduces severity.

Feline immunodeficiency virus (FIV)

InfectiousSpread by bites

A slow-acting virus, mainly spread through bite wounds, that gradually weakens the immune system — similar in concept to HIV in humans, but not transmissible to people.

Watch for: often no signs for years, then recurrent infections, dental/gum disease, weight loss and poor coat.

Treatment: no cure, but many FIV-positive cats live long, comfortable lives with good care, a stress-free indoor home, and prompt treatment of infections.

Feline leukaemia virus (FeLV)

InfectiousVaccine available

Spread through close contact, saliva and shared bowls. More serious than FIV, it can cause anaemia, immune suppression and cancer.

Watch for: persistent fever, weight loss, pale gums, poor coat and recurrent infections.

Treatment: no cure; care focuses on managing complications. Vaccination and keeping cats indoors are the best prevention.

Everyday problems

These are extremely common and largely preventable with good routine care.

Dental disease

Very commonPreventable

A huge proportion of adult cats have some dental disease — and it’s genuinely painful, even when cats keep eating.

Watch for: bad breath, drooling, red gums, pawing at the mouth, and difficulty eating.

Treatment: professional cleaning under anaesthetic, tooth removal if needed, and at-home dental care to prevent recurrence.

Feline lower urinary tract disease (FLUTD)

CommonCan be an emergency

An umbrella term for painful bladder and urinary conditions, often linked to stress, diet and hydration.

Watch for: straining in the litter tray, frequent small trips, blood in urine, crying, and urinating outside the tray.

Treatment: depends on the cause. A male cat straining but passing little or no urine may have a blockage — a life-threatening emergency. Seek a vet immediately.

Obesity

Very commonPreventable

One of the most common and most preventable problems. Excess weight raises the risk of diabetes, arthritis and other diseases.

Watch for: loss of a visible waist, difficulty feeling the ribs, and reduced activity or grooming.

Treatment: a vet-guided feeding plan, measured portions, and more play and activity.

Parasites (fleas & worms)

Very commonPreventable

Fleas and intestinal worms (such as roundworm and tapeworm) affect cats of all ages. Many cats show no obvious signs.

Watch for: scratching, flea dirt in the coat, visible worms or segments near the tail, weight loss, a dull coat, and occasional vomiting or diarrhoea.

Treatment: routine vet-recommended flea and worming products used on a regular schedule.

🚨 Get to a vet straight away if you see

  • Straining to urinate with little/no result
  • Difficulty or open-mouth breathing
  • Collapse or unresponsiveness
  • Seizures
  • Pale, white or blue gums
  • Repeated vomiting or vomiting blood

When in doubt, phone your vet or an emergency clinic and describe what you’re seeing.

How to lower the risk

You can’t prevent everything, but these habits make a real difference:

  1. Routine vet check-ups — yearly for adults, twice a year for seniors.
  2. Keep vaccinations and parasite control up to date.
  3. Feed a complete, balanced diet and keep your cat at a healthy weight.
  4. Encourage water intake and keep litter trays clean.
  5. Look after teeth with vet-advised dental care.
  6. Know your cat’s normal — weight, appetite and habits — so you spot changes early.

✓ The takeaway

Most common cat diseases are far easier to manage when caught early. You don’t need to memorise every condition — just watch for changes in drinking, eating, weight, litter tray habits and energy, and check in with your vet when something seems off.
