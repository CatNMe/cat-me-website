Foods Cats Must Avoid

A morsel from your plate can seem harmless — but some everyday human foods are genuinely toxic to cats, and a few can be life-threatening. Here are the ones to keep well out of paw’s reach, the warning signs, and what to do.

⏱️ 9 min read
Updated 7 July 2026
First published 6 July 2026
Reviewed for Cat&Me Nutrition

Cats are usually fussier than dogs about what they eat, but they still get into trouble — licking a plate, stealing a scrap, or chewing something left on the counter. Because cats are small, even a little of certain foods can cause serious harm. This guide covers the most important ones to avoid.

⚠️ If you suspect poisoning, act fast

Don’t wait for symptoms. Call your vet or an emergency clinic immediately, and if you can, tell them what was eaten and roughly how much. Never try to make your cat vomit unless a vet tells you to.

The most dangerous foods

Onions, garlic, chives & leeks

Damages red blood cells

All members of the allium family damage a cat’s red blood cells and can cause anaemia — whether raw, cooked, or powdered. Hidden forms like onion or garlic powder (in soups, sauces, baby food and seasoning blends) are especially easy to overlook.

Watch for: weakness, lethargy, reduced appetite, pale gums, and orange to dark-red urine.

Chocolate, coffee & caffeine

Heart & nervous system

These contain methylxanthines (theobromine and caffeine). The darker the chocolate, the higher the risk — baking chocolate and cocoa powder are the most dangerous, white chocolate the least. Caffeine in coffee, tea, and energy drinks is also toxic.

Watch for: vomiting, diarrhoea, restlessness, rapid or abnormal heartbeat, tremors, and seizures.

Grapes, raisins, sultanas & currants

Risk of kidney failure

The exact toxin isn’t fully understood, but these can cause sudden kidney failure. Because sensitivity varies and the risk is serious, treat all amounts as unsafe — including baked goods that contain them.

Watch for: vomiting, lethargy, reduced appetite, and reduced urination.

Alcohol & raw yeast dough

Rapidly dangerous

Even small amounts of alcohol affect cats far more than people, causing intoxication, breathing problems, and potentially coma or death. Raw bread dough is doubly risky: it expands in the stomach and ferments into alcohol.

Watch for: disorientation, vomiting, breathing difficulty, and collapse.

Raw meat, raw eggs & raw fish

Bacteria & deficiencies

Raw meat and eggs can carry Salmonella and E. coli. Raw egg white contains avidin, which blocks absorption of the B-vitamin biotin, and a diet heavy in certain raw fish can lead to a thiamine (vitamin B1) deficiency.

Watch for: vomiting, diarrhoea, and over time, skin or coat problems.

Other things to keep away

Avoid

Xylitol (a sweetener in sugar-free gum, sweets and some peanut butters), grapes/raisins in baked goods, cooked bones (splinter risk), fatty scraps (can trigger stomach upset or pancreatitis), dairy (most adult cats are lactose intolerant), mushrooms, and excess salt.

Signs of food poisoning in cats

Symptoms depend on what was eaten, but call your vet if you notice any of these — especially if you know your cat got into something.

🚨 Contact your vet if you see

  • Repeated vomiting or diarrhoea
  • Lethargy or weakness
  • Pale gums
  • Loss of appetite
  • Tremors or seizures
  • Rapid or abnormal heartbeat
  • Difficulty breathing
  • Disorientation or collapse

Keep your vet’s number and a 24-hour emergency clinic saved in your phone — minutes matter with poisoning.

Safer treat ideas

If you like to treat your cat from time to time, stick to cat-specific treats or small amounts of plain, cooked meat (no seasoning, onion, garlic or bones). Keep treats to no more than about 10% of daily calories.

Plain cooked chicken or turkey (no seasoning)Onion, garlic, chives, leeks

✓ The takeaway

When in doubt, don’t share. Keep human food off reachable surfaces, be careful with holiday leftovers, and save your vet’s emergency number. If your cat eats something on this list, call for advice straight away.
