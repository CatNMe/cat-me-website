Why Cats Need Scratching Posts

Scratching isn't your cat being destructive — it's a deep, healthy instinct. The trick isn't to stop it, but to give your cat somewhere better than the sofa to do it.

⏱️ 8 min read
Updated 7 July 2026
First published 7 July 2026
Reviewed for Cat&Me Indoor Life

If your cat shreds the arm of the sofa, they're not being spiteful — they're doing something every healthy cat is driven to do. Scratching is a completely normal, necessary behaviour that serves several purposes at once. Even declawed cats still go through the motions. So the goal is never to stop scratching; it's to redirect it onto a post your cat actually prefers. Understand why cats scratch, and choosing the right post becomes obvious.

💡 The short version

Cats scratch to stretch, maintain their claws, mark territory with scent and sight, and relieve stress. Give them a tall, sturdy, sisal post placed where they already scratch, and they'll usually leave your furniture alone.

Why cats scratch

🐱 To stretch their bodies

Digging claws into a surface and pulling back lets a cat stretch the muscles and tendons of the paws, legs, shoulders and back. It's part workout, part morning stretch — which is why cats often scratch right after waking.

✂️ To maintain their claws

Scratching removes the worn outer sheath of the claw, revealing the sharp new layer underneath. It keeps claws healthy and conditioned — grooming equipment they can't use any other way.

📍 To mark territory

Cats have scent glands in their paw pads. Scratching leaves both a visible mark and a scent signal, telling other cats “this is mine.” That's why they often scratch in prominent, well-travelled spots — and near doorways or their favourite people.

😺 To relieve stress & feel good

Scratching is a natural stress-reliever and a way to burn energy and express emotion — many cats scratch enthusiastically when excited, such as when you come home. Marking with familiar scent also helps them feel secure.

How to choose the right post

Most “my cat won't use the post” problems come down to a post that doesn't suit the cat. Look for these:

  • Tall enough to stretch fully. For a vertical post, aim for at least 30 inches (about 75 cm) so an adult cat can reach up and pull.
  • Rock-solid and stable. A wobbly post that tips or shifts will scare a cat off it for good. It needs a heavy, wide base.
  • The right material. Most cats love sisal rope or fabric — coarse and satisfying to shred and tough enough to last. Cardboard is also popular; some prefer carpet or wood.
  • The right orientation. Watch how your cat already scratches. Sofa arms and chair legs mean they like vertical; rugs and carpet mean they may prefer a horizontal pad or ramp. Offer both if unsure.

✓ Rule of thumb

Match the new post to what your cat is already ruining. If they scratch the vertical corner of the couch, a tall vertical sisal post is what they want — not a flat cardboard pad on the floor.

Placement & how many

  • Put posts where the scratching happens. Placing a post right next to the sofa corner they target works far better than hiding it in a spare room.
  • Use prominent, social spots. Cats want to mark where it counts — near sleeping areas, doorways and living spaces, not tucked out of sight.
  • Provide more than one. Have posts in the main rooms your cat uses. In multi-cat homes, provide several so cats don't have to compete — this also reduces tension.

Getting your cat to use it

  1. Place it right. Start beside the furniture they're scratching, then move it gradually once the habit sticks.
  2. Make it appealing. Rub or sprinkle catnip on it, or dangle a wand toy against it so they claw it during play.
  3. Reward use. Praise or treat your cat whenever they scratch the post so it becomes the rewarding choice.
  4. Make furniture less appealing. Temporarily cover targeted spots with double-sided tape or a throw while the new habit forms.
  5. Never punish. Shouting or spraying water just creates fear and stress — and stress can increase scratching. Redirect, don't scold.

⚠️ Please don't declaw

Declawing isn't a nail trim — it's the amputation of the last bone of each toe, and it can cause lasting pain and behaviour problems. It's banned or discouraged in many countries. Providing good posts and regular nail trims is the humane, effective alternative.

Keep it working

  • Choose a post at least 30 inches tall and very stable
  • Pick sisal (or your cat's preferred texture)
  • Offer both vertical and horizontal options
  • Place posts where your cat already scratches
  • Have one in each main room, plus extras for multi-cat homes
  • Add catnip and reward use; never punish
  • Replace posts when badly worn — but not too soon; the scent matters

✓ The takeaway

Scratching is healthy and here to stay — your job is to give it the right target. A tall, sturdy, well-placed sisal post that suits your cat's style will protect your furniture and keep your cat happy, stretched and stress-free.
