Best Toys for Indoor Cats

The best cat toy isn't the most expensive one — it's the one that lets your cat hunt. Here's which types of toys actually work, and the simple trick that makes any of them irresistible.

⏱️ 8 min read
Updated 7 July 2026
First published 7 July 2026
Reviewed for Cat&Me Indoor Life

Every indoor cat is a tiny predator with nowhere to hunt. Toys are how you give that instinct an outlet — which is why the best toys aren't about flashiness but about how well they mimic prey. A five-dollar feather on a string, used well, beats a pile of expensive gadgets left ignored on the floor. Below are the toy types worth owning, and how to use them so your cat stays interested.

💡 The short version

Own a mix of wand toys (for you and your cat together), puzzle feeders (for the brain), solo and motion toys (for alone time), and catnip kickers, balls and tunnels. Then rotate them and play like it's a hunt.

The toy types worth owning

🪶 Wand & teaser toys — the MVP

A lure on a string on a pole. This is the only toy type that lets you recreate the entire prey sequence — stalk, chase, pounce and grab — because you control it. Move it like a bird or mouse, not randomly. Best for daily bonding play. Put it away between sessions so string can't be swallowed.

🧩 Puzzle feeders & treat toys

Toys that release kibble or treats as the cat rolls, paws or solves them. They turn a meal into a job, slow fast eaters, and work minds and bodies — great for cats home alone. Start easy so your cat wins early, then make it harder.

🔋 Motion & automatic toys

Battery or robotic toys — fluttering butterflies, erratic balls, rechargeable “mice” — that move on their own. Good for solo play, especially ones with an auto-off timer. They can't fully replace human play but keep cats busy while you're out.

🌿 Catnip toys & kickers

Catnip (or silvervine) toys and “kicker” toys the cat can grab and rabbit-kick with the back legs satisfy the grab-and-bite finish of the hunt. Not all cats respond to catnip — it's genetic — so try silvervine or valerian if catnip does nothing.

⚽ Balls, mice & tunnels

Simple chase toys — lightweight balls, crinkle balls, small mice — and collapsible tunnels for ambush games. Cheap, effective, and easy to scatter around for spontaneous play. Even a scrunched paper ball works brilliantly.

📦 Free “toys” that always work

Cardboard boxes, paper bags (handles removed), and toilet-roll tubes give hiding, ambushing and shredding fun for nothing. Never underestimate a plain box.

How to play the right way

The toy matters less than the technique. To keep your cat engaged, work with their instincts:

  • Mimic prey. Move the toy away from the cat and let it dart, freeze and hide — prey never flies at a predator's face.
  • Follow the sequence. Let them stare, stalk, chase and finally catch it. Constant misses cause frustration.
  • Keep sessions short. Two or three 5–15 minute sessions a day beat one long one. Many cats are most playful at dawn and dusk.
  • End with a “kill” and a snack. Finish on a catch, then feed a treat or meal — this completes the hunt cycle and helps cats relax and sleep.

✓ The rotation trick

Cats get bored of toys they see constantly. Keep most toys in a cupboard and put out just a few at a time, swapping weekly. “New” old toys feel exciting again — and you buy less.

Match the toy to your cat

If your cat…Try
Loves to chase and sprintWand toys, balls, motion toys
Is home alone a lotPuzzle feeders, auto motion toys
Grabs and bunny-kicks toysCatnip kickers, larger plush toys
Is older or less mobileGentle wand play, ground-level toys, food puzzles
Ignores toys entirelyTry silvervine, scatter-feeding, or a plain box

Toy safety

  • Supervise string, wand and ribbon toys and store them out of reach — swallowed string is a serious emergency.
  • Check toys regularly and bin anything with loose small parts, bells or eyes that could be chewed off.
  • Avoid rubber bands, hair ties and plastic bags left out as “toys.”
  • Choose toys sized so they can't be swallowed whole.

⚠️ Watch the string

String, thread, tinsel and ribbon are among the most dangerous things a cat can swallow — they can bunch and damage the intestines. Only use string toys during supervised play and put them fully away afterwards.

✓ The takeaway

Buy for the instinct, not the gimmick: one great wand toy, a puzzle feeder, and a few chase toys will keep most cats happier than a whole toy box. Then play like prey, end with a snack, and rotate to keep it fresh.
