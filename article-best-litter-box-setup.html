Best Litter Box Setup

Most litter box “behaviour problems” are really setup problems. Get the number, size, litter and location right — and keep it clean — and your cat will happily use the box every time.

⏱️ 8 min read
Updated 8 July 2026
First published 8 July 2026
Reviewed for Cat&Me Care

Cats are naturally fastidious. When a cat avoids the litter box, it's rarely “spite” — it's almost always telling you something about the box: too few, too small, too dirty, wrong litter, or a stressful location. Fix the setup and the vast majority of problems solve themselves. Here's how to get it right from the start.

⚠️ Sudden accidents? Call the vet first

If a reliably clean cat suddenly starts going outside the box — or straining, crying, or making frequent tiny trips — rule out a urinary infection or blockage before anything else. A male cat unable to urinate is a life-threatening emergency.

How many boxes?

The golden rule from vets and feline behaviour experts is one box per cat, plus one extra — the “n + 1” rule.

  • One cat → two boxes ideally (at minimum one, kept scrupulously clean).
  • Two cats → three boxes.
  • Three cats → four boxes, and so on.

Spread them across different rooms and floors — two boxes side by side count as one “station” to a cat. In a multi-level home, put at least one box on each floor so kittens and older cats never have far to go.

Size, type and litter

Go bigger than you think

Most shop-bought boxes are too small. Aim for a box about one and a half times the length of your cat from nose to tail base, so they can turn around, dig and bury comfortably. A large, shallow storage tub often works better than a “proper” litter box.

Covered or open?

Research shows most cats have no strong preference — but hoods trap odours and can make a cat feel cornered. If you use a covered box, keep it extra clean, and always offer at least one open box too so your cat can choose.

Litter type and depth

Most cats prefer a soft, fine, unscented clumping litter. Heavy perfumes that smell pleasant to us can put cats off. For depth, cats like roughly 3 cm (a couple of inches) — enough to dig and bury without being so deep it feels unstable.

Litter typeTypical depthNotes
Clumping clay~3–4 cmEasy daily scooping; a firm favourite for many cats
Non-clumping~2–3 cmNeeds full changes more often
Crystal / silica~2–3 cmGood odour control; some diggers dislike it
Pellets / paper~1–2 cmLow dust; texture doesn't suit every cat

💡 Changing litter? Do it slowly

Cats can be fussy about texture. Switch brands gradually by mixing the new litter into the old over a week or two, so the change doesn't send them looking for the carpet instead.

Where to put the box

Location makes or breaks a setup. Cats want to feel safe and undisturbed while they're vulnerable.

  • Choose quiet, low-traffic spots — not next to a noisy washing machine or boiler.
  • Keep boxes well away from food and water bowls; cats won't toilet where they eat.
  • Avoid dead-end corners where a nervous cat could feel ambushed — they like to see who's coming.
  • Steer clear of spots by cat flaps or full-length windows, where an outdoor cat could feel watched.
  • Make sure kittens and senior or arthritic cats can get in easily — use a low-sided box for them.

Keeping it clean

Cleanliness is the number-one thing cats care about. A dirty box is the most common reason cats “go” elsewhere.

  • Scoop at least once or twice a day, ideally after use.
  • Top up litter to keep the depth consistent.
  • Do a full empty-and-wash regularly — use mild, unscented soap, not strong-smelling disinfectants.
  • Replace boxes when they get scratched and odour-soaked (usually once a year).
  • Add a litter mat to catch tracked litter and keep the area tidy.

Litter box quick checklist

  • One box per cat, plus one extra
  • Boxes spread across rooms and floors
  • Box about 1.5× your cat's body length
  • At least one open (uncovered) option offered
  • Soft, unscented litter about 3 cm deep
  • Placed somewhere quiet, away from food and water
  • Easy access for kittens and senior cats
  • Scooped once or twice daily
  • Fully washed regularly with mild soap
  • Any change of litter made gradually

✓ The takeaway

Give your cat enough big, clean boxes in calm locations, filled with a soft litter they like, and keep them spotless. Nail those basics and litter box trouble becomes rare — and any sudden change becomes an early warning worth a vet call.
