Why Cats Need Meat

Unlike dogs — and unlike us — cats can’t thrive on a plant-based diet. They are “obligate carnivores,” and their bodies are wired to get key nutrients only from animal tissue. Here’s the science, in plain English.

⏱️ 8 min read
Updated 7 July 2026
First published 6 July 2026
Reviewed for Cat&Me Nutrition

The phrase “obligate carnivore” means a cat is obliged to eat meat. It isn’t a preference or a lifestyle choice — it’s biology. Over millions of years as hunters, cats evolved to run their bodies on animal protein and fat, and they lost the ability to make several nutrients that other animals produce for themselves.

💡 The short version

Cats need animal-based protein and specific nutrients — taurine, arachidonic acid, preformed vitamin A and enough of certain amino acids — that are found in meat, not plants. A complete cat food is built around this.

Built to run on protein

Cats use protein not just for building and repairing the body, but as a core energy source — much more so than dogs or humans. Their metabolism is set to break down protein continuously, even when dietary protein is scarce. That’s why cat food is protein-rich, and why a low-protein or plant-only diet can leave a cat malnourished even if their tummy is full.

They also have little use for large amounts of carbohydrate. Cats can digest some carbs, but their bodies are simply not designed to rely on them the way omnivores do.

The nutrients cats can only get from animals

Taurine

An essential amino acid cats can barely make themselves. It’s found in animal tissue and is vital for the heart, vision, digestion and reproduction. A long-term taurine deficiency can cause retinal degeneration (blindness) and a serious heart condition called dilated cardiomyopathy — which is why every complete cat food is supplemented with it.

Arachidonic acid

An essential fatty acid that cats can’t produce in adequate amounts. It supports skin, coat, and healthy inflammation responses. It occurs in animal fats — not in plant oils.

Preformed vitamin A

People and dogs can convert beta-carotene from plants into vitamin A. Cats can’t do this efficiently, so they need the ready-made (“preformed”) vitamin A found in animal sources like liver.

Other key nutrients

Cats also depend on animal sources for enough of the amino acids arginine (critical for clearing waste from the body) and methionine/cysteine, plus vitamin B12 and niacin. Their bodies simply aren’t built to synthesise these from plants.

What about vegetarian or vegan diets?

Because of everything above, plant-only diets are risky for cats and can lead to serious deficiencies. This is a key difference from dogs, which are more flexible omnivores. If you ever have ethical or medical reasons to alter your cat’s diet, do it only under the guidance of a veterinary nutritionist who can ensure every essential nutrient is covered.

⚠️ Don’t DIY a meat-free (or homemade) cat diet

Getting feline nutrition wrong causes real harm — from taurine-related blindness and heart disease to dangerous deficiencies. Complete, balanced commercial foods are formulated to prevent exactly this.

What this means for the bowl

You don’t need to feed raw or hunt for exotic meats — you just need a properly formulated food built around animal protein.

  • Choose foods labelled “complete and balanced” for your cat’s life stage — this guarantees taurine and the other essentials.
  • Look for a named animal protein (chicken, turkey, fish, etc.) as a main ingredient.
  • Don’t rely on human meat scraps as a full diet — plain cooked meat is fine as an occasional treat, but it isn’t complete on its own.
  • Avoid raw feeding unless supervised by your vet, due to bacteria and balance risks.

✓ The takeaway

Cats are true carnivores by design. Feed a complete, balanced, animal-protein-based food and you’ll cover the nutrients — like taurine — that their bodies can’t make. When in doubt, ask your vet for a recommendation.
