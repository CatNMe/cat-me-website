How Much Should Cats Eat?

Feeding “a bowlful” is how a lot of cats end up overweight — and most owners genuinely don’t realise it. Here’s how to work out the right amount for your cat by weight, age and activity, and how to keep portions on track.

⏱️ 9 min read
Updated 7 July 2026
First published 6 July 2026
Reviewed for Cat&Me Nutrition

There’s no single number that fits every cat — it depends on weight, age, whether they’re neutered, how active they are, and the food itself. But you can get very close with a simple calorie estimate and by reading the label. It matters: surveys suggest more than half of pet cats are overweight, and many owners don’t recognise it.

💡 A useful rule of thumb

A healthy, average adult cat needs roughly 20 calories (kcal) per pound of ideal body weight per day — so a typical 10 lb (4.5 kg) cat needs about 200 kcal a day. This is a starting estimate; your vet can fine-tune it.

Start with calories, not cups

Different foods have very different calorie densities, so “half a cup” of one food is not the same as another. The reliable approach is:

  1. Estimate your cat’s daily calorie needs (see the table below).
  2. Check the calories on your food’s packaging — usually listed as kcal per cup (dry) or kcal per can/pouch (wet).
  3. Divide daily calories by the food’s calories to get your daily portion.

Base the calculation on your cat’s ideal weight, not their current weight if they need to slim down.

Rough daily calorie guide (healthy adult cats)

Ideal weightNeutered adultProne to weight gain
5 lb (2.3 kg)~155 kcal/day~130 kcal/day
7.5 lb (3.4 kg)~210 kcal/day~175 kcal/day
10 lb (4.5 kg)~260 kcal/day~215 kcal/day
12.5 lb (5.7 kg)~300 kcal/day~260 kcal/day
15 lb (6.8 kg)~355 kcal/day~295 kcal/day

These are estimates for cats at an ideal body condition. Individual needs can vary by up to 50%, so use them as a starting point and adjust based on your cat’s weight over time.

Wet, dry, or both

The calorie method works for any format. As a very rough illustration, a 10 lb cat on an all-wet diet often eats around 3–4 standard cans a day, while a dry-fed cat of the same size might eat around ½–⅔ cup a day — but always defer to the calorie count on your specific food, as brands vary a lot.

If you feed a mix, count the calories from both toward the daily total so it doesn’t quietly add up.

How often to feed, by age

Life stageTypical feeding
Kittens under 6 months3 meals a day (high energy needs for growth)
6 months to 1 yearTwice a day
Adult cats (1–10 yrs)Once or twice a day
Senior cats (10+ yrs)Usually the same routine — follow your vet’s advice

Many cats do well with small, frequent meals, which suits their natural “little and often” hunting pattern. Puzzle feeders are a great way to slow fast eaters and add enrichment.

Avoiding the overfeeding trap

Free-pouring kibble and topping up a bowl “whenever it’s empty” is the fastest route to a chubby cat. Because obesity raises the risk of diabetes, joint disease and a shorter life, portion control genuinely protects your cat’s health.

⚠️ Watch the extras

Treats, milk, and food toppers all add calories. Keep treats to no more than about 10% of daily calories, and subtract them from meals so the total stays the same.

✓ Check body condition, not just the scale

At a healthy weight you should be able to feel (but not see) the ribs, see a slight waist from above, and a gentle tummy tuck from the side. If the ribs are hard to feel, it’s time to trim portions — ideally with your vet’s guidance.

Weigh, measure, and adjust

  • Measure every meal with a proper scoop or kitchen scale — eyeballing leads to creep.
  • Weigh your cat regularly and track the trend.
  • Adjust gradually: if your cat is slowly gaining, reduce slightly; if losing unintentionally, check with your vet.
  • Make any food changes gradually over 7–10 days to avoid tummy upset.

✓ The takeaway

Feed to your cat’s ideal weight using calories, not guesswork: estimate daily needs, read the label, measure portions, and check body condition every few weeks. For weight loss or any health condition, plan it with your vet.
