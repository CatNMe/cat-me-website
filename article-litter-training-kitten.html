Litter Training a Kitten

Good news: cats are naturally clean and instinctively seek out a sandy spot to go. “Litter training” is really about giving your kitten the right box, the right litter, and a little gentle encouragement.

⏱️ 7 min read
Updated 7 July 2026
First published 7 July 2026
Reviewed for Cat&Me Kitten Care

By the time a kitten comes home — usually around 8–12 weeks — they've often already watched their mother use a tray and have the instinct to dig and bury. Your job is mostly to remove the obstacles: an easy box, a litter they like, a calm location, and praise instead of punishment.

💡 The short version

Use a shallow, uncovered tray with unscented litter, keep it in a quiet spot away from food, and gently pop your kitten in after meals and naps. Reward success, never scold accidents, and keep it clean.

1. Choose the right litter box

For a small kitten, accessibility is everything:

  • Go shallow. Low sides let a tiny kitten climb in without a struggle.
  • Go uncovered to start. Open trays feel less trapping and are easier to learn in.
  • Go big enough. A tray should be about one and a half times your cat's body length — size up as they grow.
  • Have enough trays. The rule of thumb is one box per cat, plus one extra. In a multi-level home, put one on each floor.

2. Pick the right litter

Most cats prefer a soft, fine-grained, unscented litter — heavy perfumes that smell pleasant to us can put cats off. A medium-to-fine clumping litter is a popular choice. Fill the tray a couple of inches deep so your kitten can dig and bury.

✓ Keep it consistent

Use the same litter the kitten already knows if you can, and change types gradually by mixing a little new in with the old. Sudden changes are a common reason cats reject the tray.

3. Place it well

Location makes or breaks litter habits:

  • Choose a quiet, low-traffic spot where your kitten won't be startled.
  • Avoid noisy appliances like washing machines and dryers.
  • Keep the tray well away from food and water bowls — cats don't like to toilet where they eat.
  • Make sure your kitten can always reach it easily, especially in the early days when they're confined to the safe room.

4. The simple method

  1. Show them the tray when they arrive, and after every meal and nap gently place them in it — eating and waking often trigger the urge to go.
  2. Let them dig. Don't hold them there; just give them a moment to explore and sniff.
  3. Praise success calmly with a soft voice or a small treat right after they use it.
  4. Repeat consistently for the first week or two — most kittens get it very quickly.

⚠️ Never punish accidents

Rubbing a kitten's nose in a mess or scolding them only creates fear and makes toileting problems worse. Cats don't learn from punishment — they learn from a clean, appealing tray and positive reinforcement.

5. Fixing accidents

If your kitten goes outside the box, work through the usual culprits:

  • Clean thoroughly with an enzyme cleaner so no scent lingers to draw them back to the spot.
  • Add a tray where they keep going, or move a tray closer.
  • Check the setup — too deep, scented, covered, or hard to reach can all deter a kitten.
  • Keep it cleaner — scoop at least daily; many cats refuse a dirty tray.
  • Reduce the space — if they're roaming too much too soon, go back to the safe room for a bit.

⚠️ When to call the vet

Straining, crying in the tray, blood in urine, or a sudden change in toileting can signal a urinary or digestive problem. If accidents persist despite a good setup, book a vet check to rule out a medical cause.

✓ The takeaway

Give your kitten an easy, clean, well-placed tray and gentle encouragement, and litter training usually sorts itself out within days. Keep it clean and praise the wins.
