Are Cats Hypoallergenic?

It's one of the most common questions from cat lovers with allergies. The honest answer: no cat is truly hypoallergenic — but some produce fewer allergens, and there's a lot you can do to live more comfortably with a cat.

⏱️ 8 min read
Updated 8 July 2026
First published 8 July 2026
Reviewed for Cat&Me

“Hypoallergenic” means less likely to cause an allergic reaction — not allergen-free. And here's the key fact: every cat produces allergens, so there is no truly hypoallergenic cat. But allergen levels vary a lot between individual cats, and simple management steps can make a real difference. Let's break it down.

💡 The one-line answer

No cat is truly hypoallergenic. All cats produce the allergen Fel d 1, but some produce less than others, and allergy sufferers can often reduce their symptoms with the right precautions.

What actually causes cat allergies?

Most people assume cat hair is the problem — but the real culprit is a tiny protein called Fel d 1. It's produced mainly in a cat's saliva and skin (sebaceous) glands. When a cat grooms, the protein spreads onto its fur and skin flakes (dander). As the cat sheds, these particles float into the air and settle on surfaces.

Fel d 1 is lightweight and sticky, so it becomes airborne easily, clings to clothes and furniture, and can even be found in homes and places that have never had a cat. It's the major allergen for the vast majority of people allergic to cats.

⚠️ It's not about hair length

Because the allergen comes from saliva and skin — not the hair itself — a long-haired cat isn't automatically “worse” than a short-haired one. Even hairless breeds like the Sphynx still produce Fel d 1.

Why some cats trigger fewer reactions

All cats make Fel d 1, but the amount varies widely from cat to cat. Some factors that influence allergen levels:

FactorEffect on allergens
SexMale cats tend to produce more than females
Neuter statusUnneutered males produce the most; neutering reduces it
AgeKittens tend to produce fewer allergens than adults
Coat colourSome studies suggest darker cats may produce more (evidence is mixed)
Individual geneticsLevels vary greatly between cats, even of the same breed

“Low-allergen” breeds

Some breeds are often marketed as more allergy-friendly because they tend to produce less Fel d 1 — though no breed is guaranteed. Commonly mentioned examples include:

  • Siberian — frequently cited as producing lower Fel d 1
  • Balinese and Javanese
  • Oriental Shorthair
  • Devon Rex and Cornish Rex
  • Sphynx (hairless, so less dander spread on fur — but still produces the protein)

✓ Test before you commit

Since reactions are so individual, the best approach is to spend time around the specific cat (or breed) before adopting, to see how your body responds. A female or neutered cat may also be a gentler choice for allergy sufferers.

How to reduce cat allergens at home

If you love cats but react to them, these evidence-backed steps can significantly lower allergen exposure:

  • Neuter/spay your cat — this reduces allergen production
  • Wash your hands after petting, and avoid letting the cat lick your skin
  • Keep the cat out of the bedroom, especially off the bed
  • Use a HEPA air purifier and vacuum with a HEPA filter regularly
  • Wipe your cat with a damp cloth or pet wipe; groom it outdoors if possible
  • Wash bedding, throws and soft furnishings often; reduce heavy fabrics and carpets
  • Clean surfaces with a damp cloth to pick up sticky Fel d 1
  • Ask your doctor about allergy treatments (antihistamines, sprays, immunotherapy)

💡 New tools on the market

There are now special cat foods designed to neutralise Fel d 1 in a cat's saliva using an egg-based antibody, which can reduce the active allergen shed into the home. They don't eliminate allergens entirely, but may help — ask your vet if this could be worth trying.

⚠️ If allergies are severe

For people with serious asthma or severe cat allergy, no management plan is guaranteed. Always speak to your doctor or an allergist before bringing a cat home, and be honest about whether living with a cat is safe for you or your family.

✓ The takeaway

There's no such thing as a truly hypoallergenic cat — all cats produce the Fel d 1 allergen. But levels vary between individuals, some breeds tend to produce less, and a combination of the right cat plus good cleaning, grooming and medical advice can make sharing your home with a cat far more comfortable.
