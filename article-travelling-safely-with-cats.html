Travelling Safely with Cats

Most cats aren't natural travellers — but with the right carrier, a little preparation and a calm approach, car journeys and trips can be safe and far less stressful for both of you.

⏱️ 9 min read
Updated 8 July 2026
First published 8 July 2026
Reviewed for Cat&Me Safety

Whether it's a quick trip to the vet or a long-distance move, most cats find travel stressful — they're territorial creatures who like the familiar. The two keys to safe, calmer journeys are a secure carrier and preparation ahead of time. A cat that already sees the carrier as a safe den, and a journey planned around their comfort, makes all the difference. Here's how to do it.

⚠️ Never let a cat roam loose in the car

A frightened cat under the pedals or on the driver's lap is dangerous for everyone. Cats should always travel in a secure carrier, ideally seat-belted in place. It's the number-one travel safety rule.

Choosing the right carrier

Your carrier is the foundation of safe travel. Look for one that is:

  • Sturdy and secure — hard-sided or well-built soft carriers that can't collapse or be clawed open.
  • Big enough for your cat to stand, turn around and lie down — but not so huge they slide around.
  • Easy to load — a top-opening or fully-opening carrier makes getting a nervous cat in far easier.
  • Escape-proof — secure latches, since some cats learn to open flimsy doors.

Line it with familiar bedding or a towel you've stroked your cat with, so it smells like home.

Acclimate before the trip

The biggest mistake is only bringing the carrier out for vet visits — the cat learns to dread it. Instead, build positive associations well in advance:

  • Leave the carrier out at home as an everyday piece of furniture, door open.
  • Put treats, meals, toys and cosy bedding inside so your cat chooses to go in.
  • Once they're relaxed in it, take short practice drives — down the block and back — building up gradually.
  • Reward calm behaviour with treats and gentle praise after each trip.

✓ Pheromones can help

A synthetic calming pheromone spray on the bedding or in the carrier (applied ahead of time, not directly on your cat) can help many cats feel more relaxed. For very anxious cats, ask your vet — don't use human sedatives.

During the journey

  • Secure the carrier with a seatbelt on the seat or in a stable footwell so it can't tip.
  • Cover the carrier partly with a light blanket — many cats feel calmer in a den-like space — while keeping airflow.
  • Talk softly and keep the car calm and quiet; a gentle, familiar voice reassures.
  • Never leave your cat in a parked car — temperatures rise to lethal levels within minutes, causing heatstroke.
  • For multi-cat trips, give each cat their own carrier — even bonded pairs can stress each other when scared.

Food, water & litter on long trips

For long journeys, avoid a big meal right before leaving to reduce car sickness. Offer water at rest stops, and for multi-day drives bring a small litter tray to offer during breaks in a secured space. Plan a route with regular, safe stops — but never open the carrier where the cat could bolt.

ID, microchips & paperwork

💡 Plan for the worst case

The biggest travel risk is a cat escaping in an unfamiliar place. Make sure they're microchipped with up-to-date details, and consider a well-fitted harness with ID for handling outside the carrier.

  • Microchip registered with current contact details
  • Collar with ID tag (quick-release for safety)
  • A temporary tag with your destination address/phone for longer trips
  • Vaccination records and any medications packed
  • Recent photo of your cat in case they get lost
  • Your vet's number and one near your destination

Reducing travel stress

Watch for signs your cat is struggling — excessive drooling, panting, constant crying, or trying frantically to escape. If your cat suffers badly from car anxiety or motion sickness on every trip, talk to your vet: they can rule out underlying issues and, when appropriate, prescribe anti-nausea or anti-anxiety medication designed for cats.

✓ The takeaway

Safe cat travel comes down to a secure, familiar carrier, patient acclimation before you go, a calm and seat-belted journey, and solid ID in case of escape. Never let a cat loose in the car or leave them in a parked one — and ask your vet for help with severe anxiety.
