How to Groom a Cat

Cats are famously self-cleaning — but a little help from you keeps their coat healthy, cuts down on shedding and hairballs, and turns into a gentle bonding ritual. Here's how to groom your cat by coat type, without the drama.

⏱️ 8 min read
Updated 8 July 2026
First published 8 July 2026
Reviewed for Cat&Me Care

Your cat spends a big chunk of the day grooming itself, so why do they need your help at all? Because your brush reaches what their tongue can't keep up with — loose undercoat, tangles, dirt and the fur that would otherwise be swallowed and returned to you as a hairball. Grooming also gives you a weekly "health check" and, done gently, becomes something many cats genuinely enjoy.

💡 Why groom a self-cleaning animal?

Regular brushing removes loose hair, prevents mats, reduces shedding and hairballs, spreads healthy skin oils, and lets you spot lumps, fleas, wounds or weight changes early — long before they become a problem.

Start with your cat's coat type

How often and how thoroughly you groom depends almost entirely on the coat. Most cats have a coarser outer coat of guard hairs and a soft insulating undercoat — and it's the undercoat that causes tangles and shedding.

Coat typeHow oftenBest tools
Short-hairedAbout once a weekSoft-bristle brush, rubber curry/grooming mitt, fine comb
Medium / long-hairedEvery day or every couple of daysWide-tooth comb, then slicker brush; detangling comb for knots
Heavy shedders (e.g. dense double coats)More often in spring & autumn moultsDeshedding tool used gently, plus regular comb
Hairless (e.g. Sphynx types)Regular gentle skin wipes/bathsDamp cloth; they need help managing skin oils, not fur

Long-haired cats living indoors shed all year and tangle easily, so consistency matters far more for them than for a sleek short-haired cat.

How to brush, step by step

  1. Check the coat first. A healthy coat looks glossy and springs back. Look for bald patches, flea dirt, scabs, lumps or sore spots as you go.
  2. Pick a calm moment. After a meal or during a sleepy, relaxed cuddle is ideal — never right after play or when they're wound up.
  3. Brush with the grain. Work in the direction the fur grows, from head to tail, one small section at a time — including chest and belly if your cat allows it.
  4. Long coats: comb first, brush second. Use a wide-tooth comb to find and ease out tangles, then a slicker brush to finish and lift loose undercoat.
  5. Keep it short. A few minutes is plenty. Stop while your cat is still happy so the next session stays positive.

✓ Make it a happy ritual

Cats remember how grooming feels. Speak softly, blend it into a petting session, reward calm behaviour with a treat or two, and stop the moment your cat says “enough” — pushing on teaches them to dread the brush.

Dealing with mats and tangles

Mats form where the undercoat clumps — usually behind the ears, under the legs (“armpits”), on the belly and around the rear. Left alone they pull painfully on the skin.

  • Work small tangles loose with your fingers and a comb, holding the fur at the base so you don't tug the skin.
  • For a stubborn mat, gently tease it apart in sections — a little at a time over several days if needed.
  • Never cut a mat off with scissors. Cat skin is thin and tents up into the mat; it's very easy to cut them.
  • If mats are large, tight or close to the skin, let a groomer or vet clip them safely.

⚠️ Sudden matting can mean illness

A cat that stops grooming and quickly becomes matted or greasy may be in pain, overweight, or unwell (arthritic cats often can't reach their back). If a normally tidy cat gets scruffy, check in with your vet.

Nails, ears, eyes and the rare bath

Grooming is more than fur. A quick routine keeps the rest in good shape too:

  • Nails: check every few weeks and trim just the sharp tip — see our dedicated nail-trimming guide.
  • Ears: healthy ears are clean and pale pink. Wipe only the visible outer part with a damp cloth; never poke inside. Dark waxy gunk or a bad smell means a vet visit.
  • Eyes: gently wipe away crusts with a soft damp cloth, using a fresh area for each eye.
  • Baths: most cats never need one. Bathe only if your cat is very dirty, has a skin condition, or is hairless — using cat-specific shampoo and warm (not hot) water.

Quick grooming checklist

  • Brush short-haired cats weekly, long-haired cats most days
  • Comb long coats before brushing to catch tangles
  • Always brush in the direction the fur grows
  • Check skin for fleas, lumps, scabs and sore spots
  • Tease out mats gently — never cut them with scissors
  • Trim nail tips every few weeks
  • Wipe outer ears and eye corners; don't poke inside ears
  • Keep sessions short and reward calm behaviour
  • Bath only when genuinely needed, with cat shampoo

✓ The takeaway

Match the routine to the coat, keep every session short and rewarding, and treat grooming as a weekly health check as much as a beauty routine. Do that and you'll have less fur on the sofa, fewer hairballs, and a calmer, closer relationship with your cat.
